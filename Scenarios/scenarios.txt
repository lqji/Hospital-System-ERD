Appointment Cancellation
When a patient cancels an appointment, we do not delete the appointment record from the database. Deleting records is generally a bad practice because it creates audit gaps and ruins historical data. Instead, the design handles this using the Status attribute in the Appointment table. If a cancellation happens, the system simply updates the status from Scheduled to Cancelled. This allows the hospital administration to keep a permanent record of the cancellation, which is useful for tracking patient no-show rates or identifying problematic scheduling trends.

Doctor Leaving the Hospital
If a doctor resigns or retires, hard deleting their profile from the Doctor table would be disastrous. It would cascade and break all their past appointments and medical records, leaving the database in an inconsistent state. To prevent this, the design relies on a soft delete mechanism using the Is_Active attribute in the Doctor table. When a doctor leaves, the system just changes Is_Active to false. This ensures all historical medical records and bills remain perfectly intact, while the application layer knows to filter this doctor out of any future scheduling drop-down menus.

Payment Handling and Partial Payments
The system handles partial payments natively because we separated the concept of an invoice from the concept of a transaction. The Billing table acts as the master invoice for the visit, storing the Total_Amount, the Balance_Due, and the Status. The Payment table stores individual money transfers. If a patient has a 500 dollar bill and pays 200 dollars today, a new row is added to the Payment table for 200 dollars. The system then updates the Billing table, dropping the Balance_Due to 300 dollars and changing the Status to Partial. The patient can keep making payments over time until the balance hits zero, at which point the status shifts to Paid.

Service Price Changes
Hospital services change prices over time due to inflation or policy changes. If we only stored the price in the master Service table, updating a price in 2025 would retroactively alter the math on a patient's bill from 2023, destroying financial integrity. The design prevents this by using the associative Appointment_Service table. The Service table holds the Current_Price, which acts as the catalog price. When an appointment is actually billed, the system copies that catalog price into the Billed_Price attribute inside Appointment_Service. This permanently locks in the exact price the patient was charged on that specific day, immunizing historical invoices from any future catalog updates.
